Visual Audit: Kala Ghoda

A Personal Archive of Micro-Heritage

Kala Ghoda is more than just a heritage precinct; it is a living museum of Mumbai's urban soul. Named after the "Black Horse" statue that once stood here, this crescent-shaped district holds one of the dense concentrations of history in the city.

But history isn't just in the grand monuments.

This project was born out of a deep love for urban exploration—the desire to walk a city not just to traverse it, but to understand it. My goal is to slow down and document the micro-heritage that most people walk past every day without noticing.

What We Document

While the guidebooks show you the museums and libraries, this visual audit looks closer. It captures the invisible details that give the city its texture:

The Art of Lettering

From Art Deco reliefs to hand-painted street signs, Mumbai has a rich tradition of architectural typography that tells the story of the buildings' identity.

Hidden Details

Weather vanes, date plaques, carved monograms, and vernacular stone railings—the small design choices that reveal the hands of the original craftsmen.

Urban Texture

The ground beneath our feet, from 150-year-old basalt paving stones to the tram tracks that once moved the city, recently rediscovered.

Ghost Sites

Mapping places that no longer exist, like the original Fort walls (1862) or the 1883 shoreline, to see how the city has grown over time.

This map is an invitation to explore deeply. To look up at a facade, to notice the curve of a bracket, or the serif on a street name. It is a testament to how much heritage Mumbai holds in every square meter, if only we take the time to see it.
